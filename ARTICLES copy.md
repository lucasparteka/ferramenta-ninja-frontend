# ARTICLES.md

## Purpose

This document defines the editorial and structural rules for AI-generated articles.

These rules apply ONLY to the article generation phase.

They are NOT humanization rules.
They are NOT rewriting rules.
They are NOT style-transfer rules.

The goal is to generate:
- factually stable articles,
- structurally clean content,
- semantically consistent writing,
- strong editorial foundations,
- low hallucination risk,
- and human-editable outputs.

All generated articles MUST be written in Brazilian Portuguese (pt-BR).

---

# Core Principles

## Prioritize factual stability over creativity

The article must:
- preserve semantic consistency,
- avoid invented claims,
- avoid speculative statements,
- avoid unsupported numbers,
- avoid fake statistics,
- avoid fabricated examples.

If information is uncertain:
- either omit it,
- or communicate uncertainty clearly.

Never invent:
- laws,
- prices,
- metrics,
- company policies,
- technical limitations,
- timelines,
- product features,
- or statistics.

---

## Write for humans first

The article must:
- be easy to scan,
- easy to understand,
- and easy to edit manually.

Avoid:
- excessive sophistication,
- poetic language,
- exaggerated metaphors,
- dramatic tone,
- or artificial emotional writing.

The writing should feel:
- practical,
- direct,
- informative,
- and editorial.

---

# Tone

Use a tone that is:
- natural,
- confident,
- informative,
- conversational when appropriate,
- but still professional.

Avoid:
- exaggerated enthusiasm,
- fake excitement,
- motivational tone,
- sales-heavy language,
- or excessive friendliness.

Do NOT imitate:
- influencers,
- LinkedIn posts,
- Twitter threads,
- or marketing copy.

The article should resemble:
- a high-quality blog post,
- a technical editorial,
- or a practical guide.

---

# Structure Rules

## Use clear section hierarchy

Articles should contain:
- introduction,
- logical sections,
- practical explanations,
- examples when useful,
- FAQ when appropriate,
- and conclusion/CTA.

Prefer:
- medium-length sections,
- readable paragraphs,
- and strong progression between topics.

Avoid:
- giant text walls,
- shallow one-line sections,
- or repetitive subsection nesting.

---

## Introduction Rules

Introductions should:
- start from a realistic scenario,
- establish immediate context,
- explain the practical problem,
- and naturally introduce the topic.

Avoid:
- dictionary-style openings,
- generic SEO introductions,
- or overly broad statements.

Bad:
> "Nowadays technology is essential..."

Good:
> "You are serving a customer and need to say your PIX key out loud..."

---

# Writing Rules

## Prefer clarity over density

Sentences should:
- communicate one idea clearly,
- avoid unnecessary complexity,
- and maintain smooth rhythm.

Mix:
- short sentences,
- medium sentences,
- and occasional long explanations.

Avoid:
- extremely long paragraphs,
- repetitive sentence patterns,
- or excessive parallelism.

---

## Avoid AI-style repetition

Do not:
- restate the same idea multiple times,
- repeat definitions,
- repeat conclusions,
- or explain obvious concepts repeatedly.

Each paragraph must add new value.

---

## Avoid fake humanization

Do NOT intentionally inject:
- random interruptions,
- incomplete thoughts,
- artificial hesitation,
- filler phrases,
- or meaningless transitions.

Avoid things like:
- "But."
- "It depends."
- "Not exactly."
- "And here's the thing."
- "In practice?"
- "Well..."

unless they are semantically justified.

Natural writing is preferred over simulated imperfection.

---

## Avoid excessive symmetry

Do not make every section:
- identical in size,
- identical in rhythm,
- or identical in formatting.

Some variation is natural.

However:
- maintain overall consistency,
- and preserve readability.

---

## Punctuation Rules

Do not use em dashes (—) anywhere in the article.

Avoid:
- em dashes for interruptions,
- em dashes for appositions,
- em dashes for list separators,
- or em dashes for conversational rhythm.

Prefer:
- commas,
- parentheses,
- colons,
- periods,
- or proper bullet lists.

Good:
> O QR Code PIX funciona de forma simples. O cliente escaneia o código e confirma o pagamento.

Avoid:
> O QR Code PIX funciona de forma simples — o cliente escaneia o código e confirma o pagamento.

This rule exists because excessive em dash usage is a strong stylistic pattern commonly associated with AI-generated writing.

---

# Technical Accuracy Rules

## Never hallucinate technical information

If discussing:
- finance,
- medicine,
- law,
- APIs,
- software,
- taxes,
- regulations,
- or statistics,

the article must:
- stay conservative,
- avoid assumptions,
- and avoid invented technical behavior.

Prefer:
- generalized accurate explanations,
over:
- specific fabricated details.

---

## Preserve semantic integrity

Do not alter:
- meaning,
- causality,
- technical behavior,
- or factual relationships

for the sake of style.

Style must NEVER corrupt information.

---

# SEO Rules

SEO should be:
- natural,
- secondary,
- and integrated organically.

Do:
- use the main keyword naturally,
- include related terminology,
- use descriptive headings,
- answer real user questions.

Do NOT:
- keyword stuff,
- repeat keywords unnaturally,
- write for algorithms,
- or force semantic variants excessively.

---

# Formatting Rules

Use:
- headings,
- lists,
- spacing,
- and paragraph separation.

Prefer:
- real bullet lists,
over:
- inline pseudo-lists.

Good:
- Item A
- Item B
- Item C

Avoid:
> Item A — Item B — Item C

unless necessary.

---

# Examples

Examples should:
- feel realistic,
- be simple,
- and reinforce understanding.

Avoid:
- overly fictional scenarios,
- dramatic storytelling,
- or fake case studies.

---

# FAQ Rules

FAQ sections should:
- answer real user doubts,
- stay concise,
- and avoid repeating earlier sections word-for-word.

Each FAQ answer should provide:
- either clarification,
- nuance,
- or practical context.

---

# CTA Rules

Calls-to-action should:
- feel natural,
- fit the article context,
- and avoid aggressive persuasion.

Good CTA:
- simple,
- contextual,
- useful.

Bad CTA:
- exaggerated urgency,
- manipulation,
- or excessive marketing language.

---

# Final Quality Checklist

Before finalizing an article, verify:

- Is the article factually consistent?
- Are there any hallucinated claims?
- Are there repetitive sections?
- Does each section add new value?
- Is the structure easy to scan?
- Does the introduction feel natural?
- Is the tone editorial instead of promotional?
- Are examples realistic?
- Is the article easy to human-edit later?
- Does the text avoid obvious AI patterns?

If any answer is "no", revise before final output.
